p-value myths

  • p-values are statements about the observed data
  • What is the probability of a coin being heads when it is face up in your hand?

p-value myths

  • p-values provide evidence for hypotheses
  • p-values are effect sizes: smaller is better

Impact of the myths

  • Descriptions of results are suspect
  • That is, right p-value, wrong interpretation of it

p-value

  • A p-value is used to link observed data to your assumptions about population parameters.
  • p-value is a statement about population parameters, not data.
  • Its how we generalize our data to a population
  • Is what we see in the data truly in the population?

Example

  • CCLD dogs had a pre-op mean PVF of 26.87 ± 7.42%

  • and 6 months after TPLO surgery, mean PVF was 40.71 ± 4.95%.

  • For the affected limb

  • 13.84% improvement in PVF, p<0.05

Conzemius MG, Evans RB, Besancon MF, Gordon WJ, Horstman CL, Hoefle WD, Nieves MA, Wagner SD. Effect of surgical technique on limb function after surgery for rupture of the cranial cruciate ligament in dogs. J Am Vet Med Assoc. 2005 Jan 15;226(2):232-6. doi: 10.2460/javma.2005.226.232. PMID: 15706973.

Example

  • That 13.84% is real.
  • p-values do not say anything about “group differences”
  • p-values do not say that 13.84% is a big or small difference
  • They say something about hypotheses about population parameters

Hypotheses

  • Often group population means are equal
  • Often that regression coeffincets are zero
  • You need to know the hypotheses to describe the results

Smaller P-values

  • Every p-value comes from a specfic hypothesis about population parameters

  • The pre-TPLO PVF population mean equals post-TPLO PVF population mean

    • That is, truly there is 0% risk reduction

  • P-values are evidence against hypotheses. Against the assumption of 0% population risk reduction.

  • Small p-values means the assumption of 0% is unlikely to be true.

larger p-values

  • A larger p-value is not in favor of 0% risk reduction.

  • It’s an inconclusive “diagnositic test.” P>0.05 is inconclusive.

P-values

  • So, when you read an article and p>0.05, that means the authors have failed to provide evidence against a hypothesis (like for a t-test, equal population group means), Not that there is no effect, or no group differences, etc.

P-values

  • Wrong: “All the dogs showed a symmetry in the hind- and frontlimbs with no difference compared with the control group (p > 0.05)”

Entoft, Jessica, et al. “Complications and Outcome of Cranial Cruciate Ligament Disease in Small Dogs Treated with Tiny TTA Rapid.” VCOT Open 6.02 (2023): e143-e149.

  • Right: “All the dogs showed a symmetry in the hind- and frontlimbs. There was not enough evidence to show a difference with the control group (p > 0.05)”

Takeaway point:

  • P-values are statements about the relationship between the data and assumptions about population parameters, not about the data alone.

  • Conclusions from p-values are against hypotheses about population parameters, e.g. against “population means are equal.” P-values to not provide evidence for anything.

Multiplicity

  • Also called Type I error inflation, family-wise error rate

  • Type I error is a wrong p<0.05, i.e., a false positive

Multiplicity

  • The idea is that every time a statistical test is performed (using the 0.05 cutoff), there is 5% chance making an error

    • Falsely stating statistical significance

  • Those errors add up over many tests, so that with many tests there is near certainty of a false positive result.

Multiplicity

  • For example, in a study with just six p-values, there is a 26% chance that at least one is incorrectly \(\le\) 0.05.

  • With 10 p-values, there is a 40% chance that at least one is incorrectly \(\le\) 0.05.

Multiplicity

  • We want the overall study, or sections of the study (e.g., a clinical part and a genetics part separately), to have “family-wise error rate” of 0.05.

  • That is, the error rate of the study or those sections “adds up” to 0.05, not 26%

  • In a sense, studies with many, uncorrected p-values are not trustworthy

The Bonferroni correction

  • There are many ways of correcting for multiplicity.

  • One simple way is make a new cutoff. Rather than 0.05, use 0.05 divided by the number of statistical tests. If there are 4 p-values, use 0.05/4 = 0.0125 for the new p-value cutoff.

  • Often done with post-hoc pairwise tests (sometimes with the Tukey correction)

Caveat:

  • Multiplicity adjustment can be nuanced, and it’s best to get help. Nevertheless, unadjusted inferences increase the false-positive rate

  • This is a problem when reviewers ask for additional analyses, because that may change all the inferences.

    • May have to make new corrections to account for additional test, or increase Type I error

Takeaway point

  • Try and correct for multiplicity
  • addess muliplicity in the discussion section to alert readers
